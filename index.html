How resonance is measured.

Resonance is Pulp's base unit: how far a message moved its audience, and how durably. Here is what's underneath the number.

Beliefs as positions.

We represent what an audience believes as a position in a structured space, built from the language they use and the behavior they exhibit across measured discussion. Two audiences with similar positions argue similarly, resist similarly, and move similarly. The space is learned, not assumed.

Movement as measurement.

When an argument lands, positions shift. Resonance is the size and direction of that shift, read from what people say next and what they do next. Durability matters as much as distance: a shift that decays in a week scores differently from one that holds, which is why the panel keeps listening after the message ends.

Why a unit matters.

Once movement has a unit, arguments become comparable across audiences, contexts, and time, and the rest of the platform, scoring, simulation, and strategy, can run on a common currency. The full formalism is in the papers.

Making causal claims carefully.

"Your message moved them" is easy to say and hard to know. This page is how we keep ourselves honest.

The problem with before-and-after.

Most measurement compares opinion before a message to opinion after and credits the message with the difference. But audiences are always moving. A campaign that ran during a news cycle inherits the news cycle's effect, and a before-and-after snapshot cannot tell the two apart.

Trajectory as the baseline.

Because Pulp measures continuously, every audience has a trajectory: the path its beliefs were already on. The question stops being "did opinion change" and becomes "did opinion depart from its path when the message arrived." That departure, observed under the panel's known conditions, is what we are willing to call an effect.

Uncertainty, shown.

Effects come with intervals, not just point estimates, and when the evidence cannot support a causal read, the output says so. A measurement company that hides its uncertainty is a storytelling company. We'd rather be checked than believed.

Inside the typology.

Before a conversation can be scored, every statement in it has to be something. The typology is the set of things a statement can be.

The categories.

Claims assert. Evidence supports. Values anchor. Narratives connect. Appeals move. Each statement in a measured conversation is classified into the typology, and each category behaves differently in the models: evidence shifts positions differently than appeals do, and the difference is part of the measurement.

Why classification comes first.

Without a typology, two analysts can read the same exchange and count different things, and a model trained on their labels inherits the disagreement. A shared typology is what makes Pulp's measurements reproducible: the same conversation, scored twice, yields the same structure.

Built from argument, refined by data.

The typology began in the practice of competitive debate and rhetorical theory, and it has been revised against thousands of measured exchanges since. It is a living standard, versioned like software, because the ways people argue keep evolving too.

The objective taxonomy.

A score only means something relative to a goal. These are the goals Pulp scores against, and why you declare them instead of letting the machine guess.

The four objectives.

Persuade: move a position. Inform: transfer understanding. Reassure: hold a position against pressure. Close: convert a position into action. Most real communication mixes them, and the taxonomy allows composites, but the score is always decomposable into how the exchange performed against each.

Declared, not inferred.

We could guess your intent from the transcript. We don't, for a reason: intent inferred by a model is unauditable, and a score against an unauditable goal is a story. You declare the objective; the declaration becomes part of the record; and anyone reviewing the score can see exactly what it measures. Boring, and the foundation of trust.

What this enables.

Once objectives are explicit, the same library of conversations can answer different questions: which arguments persuade, which explanations inform, which framings hold under attack. One dataset, four lenses, all auditable.

How conversations become maps.

A transcript is a sequence. An argument is a structure. Mapping is the step between them.

Nodes and relations.

Every classified statement becomes a node: a claim, a piece of evidence, a value, an appeal. The relations between them, supports, attacks, concedes, reframes, become edges. What was a wall of text is now a graph with shape: you can see where an argument is load-bearing and where it is decoration.

One artifact, two readers.

The same map is what the models score and what an analyst can open. That shared artifact is the point: when a number looks surprising, you can trace it back through the map to the exact exchange that produced it. Measurement you can walk into is measurement you can trust.

Where the shape pays off.

Structure is what makes arguments comparable. Two debates on different topics can share a skeleton, the same attack on the same kind of evidence, and the map is what lets the models recognize the rhyme. That recognition is where the learning compounds.

Modeling the argument itself.

Most language AI reads what was said. Pulp models what was argued.

Text versus argument.

A transcript is words in order. An argument is positions, attacks, support, and movement. Modeling the second requires structure the first does not show.

Why preparation came first.

The structure had to be defined before it could be learned: a typology, a mapping discipline, and the scored data to train on. The trick is the preparation.
